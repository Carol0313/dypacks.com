# Why Your MOQ Matters: How Order Quantity Affects Packaging Unit Cost

> **Sub-header:** Understanding MOQ is the fastest way to optimize your packaging budget.

---

## Introduction

One of the most common questions we hear from brands is: "Why does my packaging cost so much per unit at 500 pieces, but drop dramatically at 5,000?"

The answer is almost always MOQ, or Minimum Order Quantity. But MOQ is not just a factory rule. It is a reflection of how packaging costs are structured. Understanding it can help you plan smarter, negotiate better, and avoid the frustration of a quote that does not match your expectations.

If you are serious about controlling your packaging budget, you need to understand what drives unit cost and where the breakpoints are.

---

## 1. Fixed Costs vs. Variable Costs

Packaging costs fall into two categories.

**Fixed costs** happen once, regardless of how many units you produce:

- Design and dieline development
- Cutting dies
- Printing plates
- V-grooving blades
- Hot stamping or embossing dies
- Machine setup time

**Variable costs** scale with quantity:

- Raw materials (paperboard, greyboard, wrap, inserts)
- Ink and laminate
- Labor for assembly and finishing
- Protective outer cartons for shipping

At low quantities, fixed costs make up a large percentage of the unit price. At high quantities, they are spread across many units and barely matter.

---

## 2. Why MOQ Exists

Factories set MOQs because setup is expensive. Every time a machine stops and starts, time and labor are consumed. A production run of 500 units uses almost the same setup labor as a run of 5,000 units.

For the factory, small orders are inefficient. For the brand, they are expensive per unit. That is why MOQs exist: to ensure both sides get a viable economic outcome.

Typical MOQ ranges for custom packaging:

| Packaging Type                      | Typical MOQ   | Notes                             |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------- | --------------------------------- |
| **Folding Carton**                  | 1,000 - 3,000 | Lower setup, faster production    |
| **Rigid Box**                       | 500 - 2,000   | Higher labor, more manual steps   |
| **Luxury Rigid Box with Finishing** | 1,000 - 3,000 | Dies and setup for foil/embossing |
| **Custom Inserts / Trays**          | 1,000+        | Mold or die costs apply           |

---

## 3. The Unit Cost Curve

Imagine a custom rigid box with $800 in total fixed costs and $2.00 in variable cost per unit.

| Quantity | Fixed Cost Per Unit | Variable Cost Per Unit | Total Unit Cost |
| -------- | ------------------- | ---------------------- | --------------- |
| 500      | $1.60               | $2.00                  | $3.60           |
| 1,000    | $0.80               | $2.00                  | $2.80           |
| 3,000    | $0.27               | $2.00                  | $2.27           |
| 5,000    | $0.16               | $2.00                  | $2.16           |

The difference between 500 and 1,000 units is dramatic. The difference between 3,000 and 5,000 is much smaller. This is why understanding your breakpoints matters.

---

## 4. Client Case Study: A Candle Brand's Ordering Strategy

We worked with "Ember & Oak," a candle brand that initially ordered 500 rigid boxes per batch to "save money" by avoiding inventory. Their unit cost was $4.80.

After analyzing their sales velocity, we suggested they order 2,000 units per run. Their unit cost dropped to $3.20. Even though their upfront spend was higher, their cost per candle sold dropped by **33%**.

The extra inventory also gave them flexibility. They no longer risked running out of packaging during their busiest season, and they could negotiate better shipping rates by consolidating orders.

---

## 5. Knowledge Pop-up: Sampling, Storage, and Cash Flow

MOQ decisions are not just about unit cost. They also affect other parts of your business.

- **Sampling:** Always order physical samples before committing to a large run. A $200 sample can save thousands in mistakes.
- **Storage:** Higher quantities require warehouse space. Factor in storage costs, especially for bulky rigid boxes.
- **Cash Flow:** Larger orders tie up more cash upfront. Balance the lower unit cost with your working capital needs.
- **Design Stability:** Only commit to larger quantities once your packaging design is finalized. Rebranding before you use 3,000 boxes is painful.
